## 3. Regulatory Actions Involving Both Entities

### CFTC Settlement (January 3, 2022)

- **Respondent:** Blockratize, Inc. d/b/a Polymarket.com
- **Violations:** Operating unregistered facility for event-based binary options (swaps under CEA)
- **Penalty:** $1.4 million civil monetary penalty
- **Requirement:** Wind down non-compliant markets, cease violations
- **Result:** International exchange operations transferred to Adventure One QSS

### FBI Investigation (November 2024 - July 2025)

- **Nov 13, 2024:** FBI executed search warrant at Coplan's Manhattan apartment, seized phone and devices
- **Focus:** Whether Polymarket allowed US users to access international platform (violating CFTC settlement)
- **No arrest or charges filed**
- **Jul 15, 2025:** DOJ and CFTC formally dropped investigations without action (Trump administration)

### Ontario Securities Commission (April 2025)

- **Respondents:** Blockratize Inc. **AND** Adventure One QSS Inc. (jointly)
- **Violation:** Offering binary options to Ontario residents
- **Penalty:** $200,000 fine + $25,000 costs + $22,966 voluntary payment = ~$248,000 total
- **Critical precedent:** Ontario regulators treated both entities as **co-operators**, not separate independent companies. This undermines the "independent operator" narrative.

### CFTC Current Posture (January-February 2026)

- **Jan 29, 2026:** Chairman Selig withdrew proposed ban on sports/politics event contracts, announced pro-prediction-market rulemaking
- **Feb 17, 2026:** CFTC filed Ninth Circuit amicus brief asserting exclusive federal jurisdiction over prediction markets
- **Active rulemaking:** New regulatory framework for event contract markets in development
- **Implication:** CFTC is currently friendly but writing new rules. The Adventure One structure may face new scrutiny under whatever framework emerges.

## 4. The "Independent Operator" Question

The central question raised by the Sportico investigation: **Is Adventure One QSS a genuinely independent operator, or a corporate formality?**

### Evidence Suggesting Genuine Independence

- Adventure One was incorporated before the CFTC settlement (Oct 2021 vs Jan 2022)
- Initial directors (Munoz, Camargo, Sarmiento) appear unconnected to Polymarket
- Polymarket's website states: "the international platform is not regulated by the CFTC and operates independently"

### Evidence Suggesting Intertwined Operations

- Polymarket CEO is an Adventure One director
- Polymarket employee is Adventure One president
- Zero identifiable Adventure One employees
- No separate LinkedIn presence, job postings, or operational footprint
- Same PR team, same social media accounts for both exchanges
- Ontario regulators treated them as co-operators
- ZoomInfo lists them as the same company
- Website is a two-line placeholder since 2023
- Adventure One's email domain and communications are unclear (no response to Sportico)

### The Legal Test

Federal regulations define "principal place of business" as:

> "where they are incorporated and the location from which the officers, partners, or managers of the legal person primarily direct, control, and coordinate the activities of the legal person."

If the president (Jones) and a director (Coplan) both work from NYC for Polymarket, the argument that the principal place of business is Panama is weak.

Former CFTC Chairman Berkovitz (2020 supporting statement, Plutus Financial sanctions): argued the agency holds "authority" to act when "conduct occurs in the United States, regardless of the location or status of the counterparties" -- including when participants are solely "non-U.S. retail customers."

## 5. Open Research Questions

### Corporate Structure Questions

1. **What percentage stake does Blockratize Inc. hold in Adventure One QSS Inc.?** Panama filings referenced by Sportico may contain this but it's not reported. If Blockratize is the sole or majority shareholder, the "independence" argument collapses entirely.

2. **Who are the original directors (Munoz, Camargo, Sarmiento) and where are they now?** Are they associated with the Garcia de Paredes law firm as standard nominee directors? Are they still involved? Are they connected to other crypto entities using similar Panama structures?

3. **What is Garcia de Paredes law firm's history with crypto/blockchain incorporations in Panama?** Is Adventure One one of many similar shell structures they've set up? This could reveal a pattern of Panama corporate vehicles for crypto companies evading US regulation.

4. **Does Adventure One maintain any actual operations in Panama?** Office space, local employees, servers, bank accounts? Or is it purely a legal entity with mail forwarded to NYC?

5. **What are Adventure One's financial statements?** Does it generate revenue independently? Does Polymarket pay it licensing/operator fees, or does money flow the other direction?

6. **How does the QCEX acquisition affect the Adventure One structure going forward?** Now that Polymarket has a legitimate US license, does Adventure One become unnecessary? Or does the dual structure (US + international) persist indefinitely?

### On-Chain / Technical Questions

7. **Does Adventure One hold any on-chain admin keys or multisig positions for Polymarket's smart contracts on Polygon?** If the "operator" has no contract-level authority, what does "operating" the exchange actually mean?

8. **Who controls the admin/deployer keys for Polymarket's CTFExchange, NegRiskAdapter, and ConditionalTokens contracts?** Are those keys held by Blockratize employees, Adventure One, or a multisig? This is verifiable on-chain.

9. **Does the Adventure One structure have any relationship to UMA oracle governance?** Do Adventure One or its officers hold UMA tokens? Vote in disputes? This connects directly to our voter conflict-of-interest research.

10. **Will Polymarket US (QCEX) use the same smart contracts and infrastructure as the international exchange, or deploy separate contracts?** If same contracts, the "independent operator" fiction is even thinner. If separate, how does liquidity fragment?

### Regulatory / Legal Questions

11. **Can other jurisdictions follow Ontario's precedent?** The EU (MiCA), UK (FCA), Singapore (MAS), Australia (ASIC) could all potentially treat Blockratize and Adventure One as co-operators. What's the exposure surface?

12. **How does the CFTC's upcoming rulemaking interact with the Adventure One structure?** If new rules require registered operators, does Adventure One need to register? Can it, given its lack of substance?

13. **What did the FBI's investigation specifically find about the Adventure One structure?** The case was dropped, but the findings were never disclosed. Were FOIA requests filed for the search warrant affidavit?

14. **Does the Berkovitz precedent (2020 Plutus Financial) apply to Polymarket's situation?** His opinion argued CFTC jurisdiction even when all customers are non-US, if conduct occurs in the US. With Adventure One's leadership in NYC, this seems directly applicable.

15. **How do other prediction market operators (Kalshi, etc.) view Polymarket's Panama structure?** Kalshi has publicly criticized Polymarket's regulatory approach. Do they or others raise the Adventure One structure in regulatory filings or lobbying?

### Token / Governance Questions

16. **If Polymarket launches a POLY token, which entity issues it?** Blockratize (US securities law implications), Adventure One (Panama -- fewer restrictions but less credibility), or a new entity (another layer of corporate complexity)?

17. **How does token governance interact with the operator structure?** If POLY token holders govern resolution, does Adventure One's "operator" role become purely vestigial? Or does Polymarket try to maintain operator control despite decentralized governance?

18. **What is the token launch jurisdiction strategy?** Polymarket's three-entity structure suggests they're aware of jurisdictional arbitrage. A POLY token launch likely requires yet another jurisdictional decision (Cayman Islands foundation? Swiss association? BVI?).

19. **Does Polymarket's existing investor base (ICE, Founders Fund, etc.) influence the token strategy?** ICE invested in Blockratize equity at $9B valuation. A token launch could dilute that equity value unless structured carefully. How does this tension resolve?

### Strategic Intelligence Questions

20. **Is Polymarket actively shopping for a new L1/L2?** Our research on UMA replacement assumes Polymarket is looking for alternatives. Are there signals they're evaluating Aptos, Sui, Solana, or others? Conference talks, GitHub activity, hiring patterns, partnership announcements?

21. **What is Polymarket's timeline for moving off UMA?** They integrated Chainlink in September 2025, filed the POLY trademark February 4, 2026. This suggests active work on UMA replacement. What's the expected timeline?

22. **Who at Polymarket is the decision-maker for infrastructure/chain migration?** Coplan (CEO), Jones (Global Affairs), CTO, or an engineering lead? Understanding the decision chain is critical for any business development approach.

23. **How do Polymarket's recent hires signal their technical direction?** Are they hiring Move developers, Solidity developers, Rust developers? This reveals which chains they're evaluating.

24. **What is Polymarket building internally vs. seeking from partners?** The POLY trademark suggests they want to own the token. But do they want to build oracle infrastructure themselves, or partner with an ecosystem that provides it?

## 6. Implications for Aptos Prediction Market Strategy

### The Panama Structure as a Sales Argument

The Adventure One structure represents a **governance and trust deficit** that technical improvements alone can't solve. This changes how we should frame the Aptos value proposition:

**Old frame (technical):** "Aptos is faster, cheaper, and more reliable than Polygon."

**New frame (existential risk):** "Your corporate structure is a regulatory liability that every investigative journalist, securities regulator, and competitor can exploit. Genuine on-chain decentralization on Aptos makes the 'who operates this?' question disappear."

### Specific Value Propositions That Address Panama Risks

| Panama Problem | Aptos Solution | How |
|---|---|---|
| "Operator" with zero employees | Protocol-level operation, no human operator needed | Market creation, trading, and resolution all execute on-chain via Move smart contracts |
| CEO as director of shell company | DAO governance via POLY token | No individual needs to be "president" of anything -- token holders govern |
| Ontario treated both entities as one | Genuinely separate protocol vs. interface | Protocol is permissionless on Aptos L1; interface companies can be in any jurisdiction |
| CFTC "principal place of business" test | No principal place of business | Aptos validators are globally distributed; no single jurisdiction controls the protocol |
| No transparency into operator decisions | Full on-chain transparency | Every market resolution, every vote, every governance action is on-chain and auditable |
| Voter conflict of interest | On-chain position tracking | `poly_oracle.move` prevents voting on markets where voter holds positions (see `ORACLE-AND-PRIVACY-DESIGN-REVIEW.md`) |

### Timing Considerations

The window for this pitch is narrowing:

1. **CFTC rulemaking is happening NOW** (Chairman Selig's January 2026 announcement). Rules being written in 2026 will lock in the regulatory framework for years.
2. **POLY trademark filed February 4, 2026.** Polymarket is actively working on token strategy. Decisions may already be in progress.
3. **Polymarket US (QCEX) is launching.** The dual US + international structure is being formalized. Migration decisions will be harder after launch.
4. **Sportico article just published.** Polymarket will be doing damage control. This is a moment where they're receptive to "here's how you fix the narrative."
